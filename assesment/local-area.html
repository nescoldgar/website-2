About Melrose

Melrose is a charming town located in the Scottish Borders, known for its picturesque town centre that seamlessly blends historical significance with natural beauty. The town's heart is marked by its cobbled streets, lined with traditional stone buildings, independent shops, cozy cafes, and inviting pubs. At the centre is the striking Melrose Abbey, a ruined yet captivating medieval abbey dating back to the 12th century, with intricately detailed stonework and a peaceful atmosphere that draws visitors from around the world. The town square, with its wide open space, offers a pleasant place to relax, while surrounding streets lead to a variety of small boutiques, artisan shops, and galleries showcasing local crafts. The town centre also features a blend of Georgian and Victorian architecture, with elegant facades and a welcoming feel. Melrose has a strong sense of community, and the town centre is a hub for local events and activities, such as markets, festivals, and cultural celebrations. Beyond the shops and cafes, the stunning natural surroundings of the Scottish Borders come into view. The nearby Eildon Hills provide a scenic backdrop to the town, and the River Tweed runs through the area, adding to the serene atmosphere. Whether exploring the historic sites, enjoying local produce, or simply strolling along the riverbanks, Melrose offers a delightful experience for visitors.

Melrose and its historic farming

Melrose's agricultural heritage dates back to at least the Roman era, though it is during the early medieval period that it began to gain prominence. The foundation of Melrose Abbey in 1136 by King David I marked a significant turning point in the town's development. By the 16th and 17th centuries, Melrose and the surrounding Borders region had become famous for its sheep farming. The Tweed Valley's lush meadows were well-suited to raising sheep, and the wool produced here became a highly sought-after commodity. The introduction of the Border Collie dog in the 19th century further revolutionized sheep farming by aiding in the herding of large flocks across the rugged terrain.

The region’s farming community faced various challenges, including periodic crop failures, the impact of harsh winters, and, in more recent times, changing agricultural policies and practices. However, the people of Melrose have continually adapted, embracing innovations in farming techniques and sustainable practices. This resilience is still evident in the region’s modern farming community, which combines traditional practices with newer agricultural technologies.

Today, Melrose remains closely linked to farming, with a strong emphasis on sheep farming, beef production, and cereal crops. The town hosts the famous Melrose Agricultural Show, celebrating local farming traditions and showcasing the skills of local farmers.
